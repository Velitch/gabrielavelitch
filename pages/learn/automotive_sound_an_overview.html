AUTOMOTIVE IMMERSIVE SOUND

An Overview



The purpose of this handbook is to bring notions, tips and possible solutions litteraly to the hand of the sound engineers while approaching to the automotive immersive sound tuning. This text is an historycal and technical summary of the most relevant articles, books, papers and general notions related to acoustics, sound engineering and sound design. We will frequently refer to the material mentioned above; it's not a purpose of this book to leave the readers fully informed about all the touched topics, infact we will frequenctly redirect them to the original source for futher studies. On the other hand, this text will focus over results, solutions, conclusions and perspecives in order to give an immediate view of the state of arts and to provide a practical approach to the matter.

Stage 1: Acoustic Fingerprint of the Vehicle


During the first stage of the soundscape design developement the engineers will capture all the usefull in-situ and open road sounds of the vehicle. Specifing the targets of those captures is essential to guarantee a smooth and on focus study during the stage 2.

Definition of Automotive Sound Targets

Some of the most important automotive sound targets are listed below:

  • Vehicle Acoustic Enviroment: impulse responses of the cabin
  • Vehicle Physical Sounds: tier/road sound, aerodynamic noise
  • Vehicle Mechanical Sounds: ICE engine noises, vehicle speed, throttle opening, gearswitch, air-conditioning, doors opening and other gadget sounds
  • Vehicle Electric Sounds: EV engine noises, battery cooling
  • Vehicle External Sounds: engine noises, tier/road

Impulse Response Capture

The frequency response allows to view both the module spectrum and the phase spectrum. By obtaining the frequency response, we actually cut out all that part of the transfer function that concerns time, i.e. the transient of that system. Therefore should be underline that the frequency response gives the behavior of the filter in stationary conditions, i.e. when some time has passed and the system has settled.

In summary, the approach to the study of an environment through the analysis of its natural modes is true and is valid for the stationarity conditions of that system, i.e. when sufficient time has passed for the family of standing waves to have established itself at interior between the two walls. The impulse response still has all the information of the system, but when obtaining the frequency response, only those relating to the stationary part of the system can be seen.

Multichannel Recording Solutions

Multichannel solutions have been developed over the years that can be categorized in three approaches:

  • Direct: those methods are the most commonly used by sound engineers and consist in manually arranging microphones and loudspeakers based on heuristics. Those methods will not be further detailed here because microphone and loudspeaker layouts are interdependent.
  • Non-parametric: those methods usually use linear transformation such as ambisonic. Input channels are encoded in the spherical harmonic domain and can further be decoded on any loudspeaker configuration
  • Parametric: those methods process microphone array signals in the time-frequency domain and estimate spatial parameters based on sound field models. Parametric techniques are mainly used in spatial audio coding because they allow to downmix the recordings and achieve good perceptual reconstruction.

Choosing which recording method to apply over each target is crucial. A good soundscape capture is based over a mix and match of different recording methods.

Statistical Analysis of the Cabin Acoustic Enviroment

The statistical analysis approach starts from the fact that the energy in a space is distributed ergotically, i.e. without particular internal organisations. The description of an acoustic response in these terms uses quantities that summarize the space in a macroscopic way and define certain properties.

The ISO 3382 standards describe the measurement procedure, the instrumentation necessary for detection, the number of measurement positions and the criterion for evaluating the collected data.
